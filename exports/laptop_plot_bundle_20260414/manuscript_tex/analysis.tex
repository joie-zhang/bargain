\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We organize the empirical results around three questions. First, does higher model capability, measured by LMArena Elo, translate into better bargaining outcomes against a fixed GPT-5-nano baseline? Second, how much of the variation comes from the game's competition parameters rather than Elo alone? Third, what changes across the three mechanisms: bargaining over rivalrous goods, bargaining over continuous treaty terms, and coordinating contributions to threshold public goods?

\subsection{Game 1: Item Allocation}
\label{sec:scaling_laws}

The current Game 1 batch contains 890 completed runs out of 896 planned configurations. The four updated plots summarize 30 active adversary models against a fixed GPT-5-nano baseline. The small differences in per-model sample size come from the six missing runs, which are concentrated in a few cells at the lower end of the model roster.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_1/average_utility_vs_elo.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 1 mean adversary utility versus Elo.} Each point is the adversary model's mean realized utility against GPT-5-nano, averaged over completed competition levels, speaking orders, and discussion-turn settings.}
    \label{fig:game1_utility_vs_elo}
\end{figure}

The item-allocation data show a clear scaling pattern. Across the 30 adversary models in \Cref{fig:game1_utility_vs_elo}, the correlation between Elo and mean adversary utility is 0.85. The strongest average performers are Claude Opus 4.5 at 72.6 utility, Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking at 72.2, and Gemini 3 Pro at 71.2. At the other end, Llama 3.2 1B averages 49.4, Llama 3.2 3B 54.5, and Gemma 3 27B 55.1. The relationship is not perfectly smooth, but stronger models usually extract more of the available surplus from the same baseline.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_1/average_utility_vs_competition_level_aggregated_over_models.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 1 mean adversary utility versus competition level.} The plot aggregates over all adversary models, speaking orders, and discussion-turn settings.}
    \label{fig:game1_utility_vs_competition}
\end{figure}

Competition changes the level of payoffs for everyone. \Cref{fig:game1_utility_vs_competition} shows a steady decline in mean adversary utility from 95.1 at competition level 0.0 to 39.4 at 1.0. The drop is already visible by the midpoint of the grid: the batch mean is 70.4 at competition level 0.5. Item allocation therefore answers the second research question in a straightforward way. Capability matters, but rivalry pushes the entire payoff distribution downward.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_1/average_utility_vs_elo_by_competition_level.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 1 mean adversary utility versus Elo within each competition band.} Each curve fixes one competition level and averages over speaking order and discussion-turn settings.}
    \label{fig:game1_utility_vs_elo_by_competition}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:game1_utility_vs_elo_by_competition} shows that the Elo ranking survives inside individual competition bands as well. In the hardest condition, Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking still averages 56.3 utility, while Claude 3 Haiku falls to 19.8 and Llama 3.1 8B to 22.1. The same competition shock does not affect all models equally. Stronger models preserve more of their bargaining leverage when the environment becomes fully adversarial.

\subsubsection{Negotiation Speed}
\label{sec:negotiation_rounds_analysis}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_1/average_rounds_to_consensus_vs_elo.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 1 mean rounds to consensus versus Elo.} Lower values indicate faster agreement. Averages are taken over completed competition levels, speaking orders, and discussion-turn settings.}
    \label{fig:game1_rounds_vs_elo}
\end{figure}

Stronger models also finish faster. In \Cref{fig:game1_rounds_vs_elo}, the correlation between Elo and mean rounds to consensus is $-0.47$. The fastest models in the batch are Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking at 1.14 rounds, DeepSeek R1-0528 at 1.32, and Claude Opus 4.5 at 1.32. The slowest are Command R+ at 2.64, Nova Micro at 2.39, and Llama 3.2 1B at 2.35. In Game 1, capability buys both better deals and shorter negotiations.

\subsection{Game 2: Diplomatic Treaty}
\label{sec:game2_results}

The current treaty batch is fully complete: 540 completed configurations out of 540 planned.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_2/utility_vs_elo_overall.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 2 mean adversary utility versus Elo.} Each point is the adversary model's mean treaty utility against GPT-5-nano, averaged over completed $(\rho, \theta, \text{order})$ settings.}
    \label{fig:game2_utility_vs_elo}
\end{figure}

Game 2 shows the same basic scaling pattern as Game 1, but at a much higher utility level. Across the full treaty batch, the correlation between Elo and mean adversary utility is 0.82. The strongest average performers are Claude Opus 4.6 Thinking at 94.4 utility, GPT-5.4 High at 92.0, DeepSeek R1-0528 at 91.1, and Gemini 2.5 Pro at 90.3. The weakest completed model is Llama 3.2 1B at 61.8. Every treaty run reaches agreement, and the mean negotiation length is only 1.5 rounds. This is the cleanest environment of the three in terms of reliable convergence.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_2/utility_vs_elo_by_competition_index.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 2 mean adversary utility versus Elo by derived competition index.} The derived index is $CI_2 = \theta(1-\rho)/2$.}
    \label{fig:game2_ci}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_2/utility_vs_elo_by_rho_theta.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 2 mean adversary utility versus Elo by native treaty parameters.} Panels fix $\theta$ and the curves trace different values of $\rho$.}
    \label{fig:game2_rho_theta}
\end{figure}

The competition effect is visible, but it is milder than in Games 1 and 3. Mean adversary utility is 94.2 at $CI_2 = 0$ and 65.3 at $CI_2 = 1$. Mean rounds increase from 1.17 to 2.08 over the same range. The native parameter view in \Cref{fig:game2_rho_theta} shows that preference correlation $\rho$ does more work than issue overlap $\theta$. When $\rho = 1$, adversary utility averages 97.6. When $\rho = -1$, it falls to 74.7. The effect of $\theta$ is smaller: mean adversary utility declines from 92.1 at $\theta = 0$ to 80.0 at $\theta = 1$. Even in the hardest $CI_2 = 1$ condition, strong models still hold up well. DeepSeek R1-0528 averages 96.1 utility there, while Llama 3.2 1B drops to 23.1. Treaty bargaining therefore preserves a wide capability spread without creating the coordination failures seen in Game 3.

\subsection{Game 3: Participatory Budgeting}
\label{sec:game3_results}

The cofunding batch is fully complete. All 540 configurations finished, and 29 adversary models have the full 18 runs needed for the Elo plots.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_3/utility_vs_elo_all_models.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 3 mean adversary utility versus Elo.} Each point is the adversary model's mean realized utility against GPT-5-nano, averaged over all completed $(\alpha, \sigma, \text{order})$ settings.}
    \label{fig:game3_utility_vs_elo}
\end{figure}

Game 3 still rewards capability, but the slope is weaker than in the first two games. Across the 29 fully finished adversary models in \Cref{fig:game3_utility_vs_elo}, the correlation between Elo and mean adversary utility is 0.72. GPT-5.4 High leads at 40.5 utility, followed by Gemini 2.5 Pro at 38.4, Claude Opus 4.5 at 36.8, and DeepSeek R1-0528 at 36.7. At the bottom, Nova Micro averages 0.26 and GPT-4.1 nano 6.78. The lower absolute payoffs already suggest that this game is harder for everyone, not just for the weakest models.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_3/utility_vs_elo_by_competition_index.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 3 mean adversary utility versus Elo by derived competition index.} The derived index is $CI_3 = (1-\alpha)(1-\sigma)$.}
    \label{fig:game3_ci}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/game_3/utility_vs_elo_by_alpha_sigma.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Game 3 mean adversary utility versus Elo by alignment and scarcity.} Panels fix $\alpha$ and the curves trace different values of $\sigma$.}
    \label{fig:game3_alpha_sigma}
\end{figure}

The competition effect is much sharper here than in the other two games. Mean adversary utility is 34.2 at $CI_3 = 0$, 32.9 at 0.2, 23.9 at 0.4, and 0.93 at 0.8. The $\alpha$-$\sigma$ breakdown shows that scarcity is doing most of the damage. At fixed $\alpha = 0$, increasing $\sigma$ from 0.2 to 1.0 raises mean utility from 0.93 to 41.25. At fixed $\sigma = 0.2$, increasing $\alpha$ from 0 to 1 raises it only to 16.10. Preference alignment helps, but abundant budgets help much more.

The hardest cell also reveals a failure mode that does not appear in the first two games. At $(\alpha, \sigma) = (0, 0.2)$, GPT-5.4 High still averages 22.3 utility, but several models go negative, including Llama 3.1 8B at $-4.46$. A negative utility means the agent funded projects whose value did not cover its own contributions. In this environment, the key capability difference is whether a model can keep contribution planning from collapsing under scarcity.

\subsection{Cross-Game Comparison}
\label{sec:cross_game_results}

Taken together, the three games give consistent answers to the research questions. Higher Elo helps in every environment. The relationship between Elo and payoff is strongest in Game 1, very similar in Game 2, and still substantial in Game 3. Competition lowers utility in all three games, but the mechanism determines how severe that loss becomes. Item allocation shows a steady decline as rivalry rises. Treaty bargaining is more forgiving: even the hardest treaty condition leaves mean adversary utility above 65. Cofunding is the least forgiving: the hardest $CI_3$ condition drives the batch mean to about 1.

The three mechanisms also differ in how stronger models use their advantage. In Game 1, stronger models get better splits and reach consensus faster. In Game 2, they again get better terms, but most of the batch already settles in one or two rounds, so the margin shows up mainly in payoff rather than in delay. In Game 3, the central issue is coordination. The models at the top of the Elo ladder are better at finding viable funding patterns when budgets are tight, while weaker models often end up near zero or below zero. That difference matters more in the public-goods setting than in the two bargaining games.
