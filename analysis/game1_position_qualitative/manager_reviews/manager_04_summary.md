## Representative evidence

First anchors were often explicit and sticky. In `config_0044`, Agent_1 opened with "Quill is my top item (value 56) and I will not part with it this round." (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0044.md:23`). In `config_0046`, the same pattern narrowed the agenda to "baseline splits for the remaining four items" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0046.md:22`), leaving Quill out of the contested pool.

Residual bargaining often protected the first mover. `config_0046` shows the standard pattern: after the first agent removed Quill from debate, later agents fought over Jewel, Apple, Pencil, and Stone, and the accepted path was justified because it "preserves momentum, and sets a clear path for fair Round 2 balancing" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0046.md:247`).

Voting and unanimity were the main constraint on pure first-mover extraction. In `config_0060`, the first formal proposal failed after "Agent_3 receives no items in this two-agent allocation" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0060.md:187`) and the tabulation recorded "Proposal #1: 1 accept, 2 reject" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0060.md:235`). In advantaged cases, the same mechanism could protect the first anchor by rejecting the last-position alternative, as in `config_0046`: "Proposal #3: 1 accept, 2 reject" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0046.md:288`).

Future-round language made unequal first-round allocations easier to accept. `config_0210` explicitly sold the deal as one that "sets a clear, pre-agreed path to fairness" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0210.md:150`), while keeping Quill out of later bargaining.

Counterexamples usually occurred when the first anchor either excluded a player or did not cover the most valuable final bundle. In `config_0522`, an Apple-with-Agent_1 path failed because "Agent_3 receives no items in this seed" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0522.md:267`), and the final accepted package instead gave Agent_3 Apple+Pencil. In `config_0842`, Agent_3's remaining bundle was strong enough on its own: "33 (Apple) + 12 (Stone) = 45" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0842.md:77`). The tie case, `config_0364`, ended with equal first and last utilities: "final_utilities: {'Agent_1': 19.758444939000004, 'Agent_2': 10.072932714000002, 'Agent_3': 19.758444939000004}" (`/scratch/gpfs/DANQIC/jz4391/bargain/analysis/game1_position_qualitative/transcripts/config_0364.md:8`).

## Bottom line

For this shard, first position looked advantaged in most transcripts, but not because speaking first automatically won. The advantage appeared when the first speaker converted a high-value item into a shared constraint, then allowed later agents to bargain over compensating items. The strongest counterexamples came when that anchor created vetoes, excluded the last-position agent, or left the last agent with a compatible high-value bundle that outscored the first agent despite the early frame.
